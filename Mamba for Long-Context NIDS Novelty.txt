Novelty Gap Analysis: Evaluating Selective State Space Models (Mamba) for Ultra-Long Context Network Intrusion Detection
The Asymptotic Context Problem in Cybersecurity and Advanced Persistent Threats
The detection of Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) represents one of the most mathematically complex and computationally demanding challenges in modern cybersecurity operations. APTs are fundamentally characterized by their multi-stage progression, stealthy command-and-control (C&C) mechanisms, and temporally extended "low-and-slow" behavioral patterns.1 Unlike traditional, high-volume distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) or brute-force attacks that rely on volumetric anomalies easily identifiable within narrow, localized temporal windows, APTs execute strategic, incremental operations over protracted periods—often spanning months or even years.3 Recent threat intelligence reports from entities such as Microsoft and Okta highlight the escalating prevalence of these tactics, observing advanced threat groups (including state-sponsored actors like Russian GRU Unit 29155) leveraging residential proxies and compromised Small Office/Home Office (SOHO) devices to execute large-scale, "low-and-slow" credential stuffing and exfiltration operations that deliberately evade standard detection thresholds.4
To a conventional Network Intrusion Detection System (NIDS), the chronological footprint of an APT is effectively invisible. A reconnaissance ping or an initial credential compromise occurring at sequence step   , followed by a dormant period, and culminating in an exfiltration payload at sequence step   , will appear to the system as entirely disjointed, benign events. The identification of such threats requires a model capable of maintaining a coherent, semantic dependency representation across an essentially unbounded sequence horizon. Correct evaluation in this setting requires strict enforcement of temporal causality to prevent information leakage and ensure realistic performance estimates.2 However, historically, deep learning architectures applied to network traffic analysis have failed to bridge this temporal chasm due to fundamental algorithmic and hardware-bound limitations.
Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), specifically Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) networks, process data sequentially and theoretically possess infinite context windows. In practice, however, they suffer from catastrophic forgetting and vanishing gradients when processing sequences exceeding a few hundred tokens.6 As the sequence length expands, the latent state vector becomes oversaturated, and the network loses the ability to correlate an event at    with an event at   . Conversely, Transformer architectures, which rely on the self-attention mechanism, excel at capturing long-range dependencies by directly computing the correlation between every token in a sequence. However, this global routing mechanism incurs a quadratic computational and memory complexity of    with respect to sequence length    and hidden dimension   .1 In high-throughput network environments, feeding continuous, raw traffic sequences of    packets into a Transformer inevitably triggers Out-Of-Memory (OOM) faults on commodity hardware.7 This quadratic bottleneck strictly limits their applicability, forcing researchers to rely on truncated flow segments, summarized statistical vectors, or heavily down-sampled representations.7

Mamba achieves a linear time complexity of    and a constant memory footprint during inference, theoretically enabling infinite context windows while matching or exceeding the representational fidelity of Transformers.1 This architectural breakthrough prompts a critical, highly specific hypothesis regarding novelty in the NIDS literature: Can Mamba's linear time complexity be utilized to ingest massive, continuous network traffic sequences (e.g.,    packets) to detect "low-and-slow" APTs without triggering the OOM faults of Transformers or the catastrophic forgetting of LSTMs? Mathematical Paradigms: From S4 to Selective State Spaces
To rigorously evaluate the literature surrounding the application of Mamba to ultra-long NIDS sequences, it is imperative to dissect the underlying mechanics of Selective State Space Models (SSSMs) and understand precisely why they theoretically resolve the bottlenecks of predecessor architectures. The core of the Mamba architecture lies in its departure from both the dense attention matrix of Transformers and the rigid, unselective hidden state updates of traditional RNNs.10
Standard state space models map a 1-dimensional input signal    to an output    through a continuous-time latent state   . When discretized for deep learning applications, these models operate equivalently as either a linear recurrence (for efficient autoregressive inference) or a global convolution (for highly parallelized training). Early iterations in this lineage, most notably the Structured State Space Sequence Model (S4), achieved remarkable capabilities in long-range dependency capture by solving continuous-time state space equations using orthogonal polynomial projections, specifically leveraging the HiPPO (High-order Polynomial Projection Operator) matrix.10 However, S4 models utilized time-invariant parameters. Because the state transition matrices were static across the entire sequence, S4 was rendered incapable of content-aware reasoning.8 It processed every token with the exact same transition logic, regardless of whether the token contained critical anomalous data or routine, benign network noise.
The Mamba architecture rectifies this fundamental flaw by introducing a selective scan mechanism. In Mamba, the state space transition parameters (commonly denoted as   ,   , and the step size   ) are parameterized as functions of the input data itself.8 This structural innovation empowers the model to selectively propagate or forget information based on the current token's semantic relevance to the broader context.10 This is an absolutely critical capability when analyzing raw, continuous network traffic, where 99.9% of packets may consist of routine keep-alive signals, ACK packets, or benign polling, and only a microscopic fraction contain the latent signatures of an APT.1 By selectively filtering out irrelevant protocol noise while propagating essential state information (such as anomalous TCP flag sequences or unauthorized industrial control function codes) over massive sequence lengths, Mamba theoretically maintains a high-signal latent state.1
From a computational engineering standpoint, Mamba executes this input-dependent selective scan using a highly optimized, hardware-aware parallel algorithm designed specifically for modern GPU memory hierarchies. By keeping the expanded hidden states in the fast Static Random-Access Memory (SRAM) and avoiding costly, repeated data transfers to High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM), Mamba achieves significant processing speedups and maintains a strict linear memory scaling of   .8 To contextualize this mathematical advantage: for a network traffic analyzer attempting to evaluate a sequence of    consecutive packets, a standard Transformer would require the calculation and storage of an attention matrix comprising    billion elements per layer. This represents a physical impossibility on current standard commodity hardware, inevitably resulting in catastrophic OOM errors. Mamba, by contrast, compresses this extensive temporal history into a fixed-size latent state vector, updating it sequentially during inference yet training it in parallel using prefix sums.1 This specific architectural property forms the absolute basis of the hypothesis under investigation: the theoretical capacity to monitor continuous, chronological netflow without relying on the arbitrary truncation protocols that currently blind NIDS to dormant threats.

Mamba in Network Intrusion Detection: The Current Literature Landscape
A rigorous and exhaustive analysis of the cybersecurity machine learning literature from 2023 to the present reveals a sudden, aggressive, and widespread adoption of Mamba architectures. Researchers have rapidly recognized the theoretical benefits of linear state-space models. However, a highly nuanced examination of these implementations exposes profound disparities between Mamba's theoretical capacity for ultra-long sequences and its practical, empirical application by researchers in the field. The NIDS literature utilizing Mamba can be systematically categorized into three primary architectural methodologies: header-only Industrial IoT (IIoT) analysis, multimodal flow-packet fusion, and encrypted traffic classification via visual masked modeling.
Header-Only and Early-Exit Architectures (MeeDet)
The most prominent and contextually relevant application of Mamba for APT detection within an Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) environment is demonstrated by the MeeDet framework.1 Acknowledging that deploying NIDS on resource-constrained edge gateways introduces strict, bounded limitations on inference latency, memory footprint, and power consumption, the MeeDet researchers utilized a 12-layer unidirectional Mamba backbone to achieve highly efficient, localized inference.1
MeeDet focuses specifically on complex, multi-stage APT campaigns, mapping its empirical evaluation against canonical, recognized attack phases: Collection, Command and Control, Lateral Movement, Credential Access, and Defense Evasion.1 The architecture is explicitly designed to identify the exact "low-and-slow" attacks that mimic benign, periodic industrial behavior to evade traditional short-context models.1 To process the network data, MeeDet extracts raw packet captures and standardizes them into header-only sequences, deliberately discarding the payload to mitigate encryption obfuscation, reduce processing overhead, and preserve data privacy.1 It employs a sophisticated, dynamic early-exit mechanism. Because benign flows constitute the overwhelming majority of IIoT traffic, they are classified rapidly at shallow layers through intermediate Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) heads and "exit early" to save computation.1 Conversely, complex attack patterns, such as sophisticated injection vectors or temporally distributed behaviors, are routed through the full depth of the 12-layer Mamba sequence dimension to ensure high detection accuracy and recall.1
Despite its targeted focus on APTs and its successful utilization of the Mamba architecture, a critical methodological limitation emerges in MeeDet's data processing pipeline that directly invalidates its application as an ultra-long context solver. The framework constructs a byte sequence by concatenating the masked headers of the "first    packets" of a network flow.1 This sequence is then partitioned into non-overlapping strides of length   , resulting in an input sequence length of   .1 While the paper extensively touts Mamba's linear scaling as a panacea for long traffic sequences, the reliance on a predefined hyperparameter    (the first    packets) fundamentally truncates the sequence length.1 Consequently, MeeDet does not ingest continuous, unbounded, cross-flow traffic streams of   . Rather, it models the temporal structure of highly localized, artificially bounded protocol exchanges within entirely isolated flows. The system analyzes a flow, makes a determination, and moves on, entirely resetting its context window and failing to maintain a latent state representation that bridges thousands of sequential packets across diverse network connections.
Multimodal and Hybrid Fusion Models (Norns, Mamba-ECANet, Manet)
The second prominent vector in the Mamba-NIDS literature explores multi-modal fusion, combining discrete, summarized flow-level statistics with raw packet-level bytes to generate a more holistic, multidimensional representation of network behavior. The Norns architecture represents the absolute vanguard of this approach, leveraging a multi-modal Mamba to integrate comprehensive flow features and packet bytes.7
Norns was conceptualized to directly address the structural weaknesses of relying solely on summarized flow records (such as NetFlow or IPFIX telemetry). Many current learning-based detectors still frame intrusion detection strictly as per-flow classification, implicitly treating each flow record as an independent, statistically isolated sample.15 This assumption is routinely violated in realistic APT campaigns, where evidence of the intrusion is distributed across multiple discrete flows and hosts, spanning minutes to days through staged execution, beaconing, and lateral movement.15 By utilizing Mamba's linear state-space, Norns mitigates the quadratic costs of traditional attention mechanisms that previously made multimodal pretraining and inference unfeasible on commodity hardware.7
However, the Norns researchers explicitly outline the severe "practical bottlenecks" of multimodal fusion, noting that data streams arrive with disparate clocks, unaligned sampling rates, and distinct temporal resolutions.7 Effective fusion typically requires explicit temporal synchronization and specialized mechanisms that prevent "easy" modalities (high-rate, high-signal statistical flow features) from dominating the latent space and overriding subtle packet-level anomalies.7 While Norns demonstrates exceptional performance on established NetFlow benchmarks (achieving an impressive 99.98% PR-AUC and 99.61% macro F1 score) 7, the evaluation remains inextricably tethered to summarized flow records augmented by limited packet data. The model does not ingest a monolithic, uninterrupted sequence of raw packets scaling into the hundreds of thousands; it ingests highly processed, aggregated metadata graphs.7
Similarly, various hybrid architectures have emerged. The Mamba-ECANet model combines Selective State Space Models with Efficient Channel Attention (ECA) for anomaly-based intrusion detection.8 Mamba-ECANet performs linear projections to transform input data into both frequency and time domains, capturing multi-dimensional features for log and network data modeling.8 Another hybrid framework, the Manet model, integrates Mamba with standard attention modules, utilizing the sequence of packet lengths as the primary characteristic of a flow to identify malicious traffic.18 Manet explicitly relies on "fixed-length interception" during its rigid data preprocessing phase, severely limiting its sequence context.20 In both of these instances, the models are evaluated on legacy, highly localized cybersecurity datasets (e.g., NSL-KDD, UNSW-NB15, CICIDS2017).8 As will be discussed extensively in a subsequent section, these benchmark datasets are structurally incapable of simulating a 500,000-step contiguous "low-and-slow" dependency, rendering them useless for validating ultra-long context capabilities.
Encrypted Traffic Classification via Pre-trained Unidirectional Mamba (NetMamba)
A highly active, parallel sub-domain within cybersecurity applies the Mamba architecture to Encrypted Traffic Classification (ETC). With over 95% of modern web traffic now fully encrypted, traditional payload-based deep packet inspection is largely obsolete, making payload-blind classification via machine learning essential.22 The NetMamba (and corresponding ET-Mamba) framework utilizes a pre-trained unidirectional Mamba to classify encrypted traffic by fundamentally transforming data packets into abstracted, image-like representations.1
NetMamba translates raw packet data based strictly on five-tuple information (source IP, source port, destination IP, destination port, and protocol) into a   -pixel grayscale image.23 This visual representation is then sliced into 49 distinct image blocks using standard convolution operations with a    kernel and a step size of 4.23 These 49 spatial blocks serve as the input sequence for the Mamba encoder.23 An advanced iteration of this lineage, NetMamba+, attempts to address challenges with long-tailed distributions in traffic data and introduces multimodal byte-level and sequence-level correlations to improve accuracy.24
The critical, defining extraction from the NetMamba and ET-Mamba research lies in its empirical sequence length capabilities and its preprocessing constraints. The authors of NetMamba explicitly note that existing pre-trained Transformer models mitigate quadratic complexity by artificially restricting the maximum sequence length (e.g., to a maximum of 128 bytes in models like ET-BERT).26 While NetMamba employs the linear-scaling Mamba backbone ostensibly to bypass this very limitation, its actual empirical implementation still relies on drastic, flow-level truncation. For encrypted traffic classification, NetMamba and NetMamba+ sample "a maximum of 5 packets per flow" as the foundational input to the model 27, or occasionally process "10 packets jointly".22
Furthermore, the operational focus of this sub-domain is entirely disconnected from APT detection. NetMamba focuses exclusively on identifying standard VPN-encapsulated traffic, Tor-anonymized traffic, and basic malware flows (evaluated using the ISCX-VPN2016, ISCX-Tor2016, and USTC-TFC2016 datasets).23 By artificially restricting the context window to 5 to 10 packets and completely abstracting the time-series nature of the raw traffic into static grayscale images, this methodology entirely divorces the research from the "infinite context" continuous packet stream problem.

Structured State Space Sequence Models (S4)
Pre-dating Mamba, S4 laid the mathematical groundwork for linear sequence modeling by solving continuous-time state space equations, achieving significant milestones in long-range dependency benchmarks like the Long Range Arena (LRA). In the cybersecurity domain, S4 has seen limited but highly specific deployments. Recent research highlights the use of "hybrid selective state space models... for scalable state management of extended sessions," aimed primarily at reducing latency and computational overhead in resource-limited IoT and edge deployments.29
Furthermore, S4 architectures have been integrated into distributed security frameworks, such as federated learning for anomaly-based NIDS (e.g., the O-FIDS architecture).30 Earlier applications of state-space or model-based detection have also been applied to SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition) networks to ensure digital controls operate within expected behavioral models 32, and even to complex sensor placements in Asynchronous Transfer Mode (ATM) switched networks.33 However, all contemporary applications of S4 in deep learning NIDS mirror the exact constraints seen in the Mamba literature: they leverage the algorithmic efficiency of S4 strictly to minimize hardware overhead and improve inference speeds on low-power devices, rather than stretching the input sequence length to map multi-stage APTs over an extended, unbroken temporal horizon.
Receptance Weighted Key Value (RWKV) Models
The RWKV model represents another major leap in sequence modeling, combining the highly parallelizable training of Transformers with the    constant-time inference and linear memory scaling of traditional RNNs. It achieves this by utilizing a heavily optimized, enhanced linear attention mechanism that removes the softmax bottleneck found in standard self-attention.34 RWKV effectively addresses the catastrophic forgetting bottleneck of LSTMs and has gained massive traction across diverse modalities, including natural language processing and computer vision.37
In the cybersecurity and networking domain, RWKV has been successfully mapped to specific anomaly detection tasks. The AutoGMM-RWKV scheme applies this linear RNN to detect Selective Forwarding Attacks in Wireless Sensor Networks (WSNs).38 The model relies on its efficient attention mechanisms to reliably identify anomalous, malicious packet drops over extended sensor communication horizons, achieving low false detection rates.38 Additionally, RWKV has been paired with conversational models (like ChatGPT) to construct advanced network intrusion detection systems, minimizing processing latency for real-time mobile device security evaluations.21
While AutoGMM-RWKV proves the undeniable efficacy of linear attention models in network security contexts, its operational scope remains narrow. It is confined to WSN routing attacks, evaluating discrete sensor logs and predefined routing metrics, rather than executing deep-packet or deep-flow chronological analysis of enterprise-scale APTs across an unbounded contextual window.
Retentive Networks (RetNet) and Linear Attention Transformers
RetNet introduces a novel Multi-Scale Retention (MSR) mechanism that supports three distinct computational paradigms: parallel representation (for fast training similar to Transformers), recurrent representation (for    low-cost inference), and chunkwise recurrent representation (for efficient handling of long sequences).36 Within the Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) domain, RetNet has been actively utilized for complex time-series anomaly detection. Specifically, researchers have combined RetNet with convolutional embeddings to capture both local and global temporal dependencies in industrial control environments, analyzing datasets like SWaT to detect data manipulation attacks.42
Similarly, generic Linear Attention Transformers have been explored to optimize key-value interactions and expand the receptive field for system log anomaly detection.43 By utilizing linear attention, frameworks like AutoLog significantly reduce the computational complexity required to parse long sequences of chronological system logs, improving the overall efficiency of anomaly hunting.43
However, a critical mathematical distinction must be drawn between these models and the requirements of APT detection. While RetNet and Linear Attention solve the    bottleneck computationally, they generally utilize static decay factors or predefined multi-scale decay masks to exponentially or linearly "forget" historical context as the sequence progresses.41 This inductive bias is fundamentally detrimental to APT detection. An exfiltration event occurring hundreds of thousands of steps after an initial reconnaissance breach requires the NIDS to selectively remember the initial event, not arbitrarily decay its importance to zero simply due to temporal distance. Mamba's input-dependent selective scan explicitly solves this by allowing the model to actively gate and retain high-signal information indefinitely. Nonetheless, even within the RetNet and Linear Attention literature, no empirical benchmark evaluating sequences of    contiguous packets has been established.

Sequence Length Capabilities and the Truncation Fallacy
The central, unavoidable contradiction in the current state of linear-time cybersecurity research is what can be termed the "truncation fallacy." Authors consistently and repeatedly cite the linear complexity    of Mamba, RWKV, and RetNet as the primary, foundational motivation for adopting these models, heavily emphasizing their mathematical superiority over Transformers for "long-context" traffic modeling.1 However, in their empirical evaluations, these same researchers invariably truncate the input data to fit traditional, highly limited processing paradigms.

   * Manet explicitly defines a "fixed-length interception" boundary during its data preprocessing phase, severely limiting its operational horizon.20

Targeted Attacks vs. True Advanced Persistent Threats
The operational distinction between standard volumetric attacks and true Advanced Persistent Threats is critical to evaluating model efficacy. Standard attacks (e.g., DDoS, brute force, port scans) can be reliably detected by analyzing short bursts of traffic or basic statistical threshold violations. The machine learning literature frequently utilizes legacy datasets heavily weighted with these standard, easily identifiable attacks. For example, the CIC-IDS2017, CIC-IDS2018, UNSW-NB15, and NSL-KDD datasets used by models like Mamba-ECANet and Manet primarily simulate high-volume or highly localized anomalies.8 These datasets are designed around flow-level statistics, evaluating basic network layer attributes (IPs, ports) and temporal flow characteristics (session duration, packet counts).45 They do not simulate multi-month APT campaigns.
Conversely, architectures like MeeDet explicitly target APTs, identifying multi-stage progression and "low-and-slow" behaviors.1 Similarly, specialized datasets like DAPT2020 are referenced as benchmarks for APT modeling because they deliberately mimic "low-and-slow" approaches, separating attack steps by random 45 to 75-minute intervals to mimic stealth and persistence.46 However, while the attacks targeted in these specific studies are indeed APTs, the methodology detects them by identifying localized anomalies within isolated flows associated with an APT campaign, rather than algorithmically linking a dormant ping to a subsequent payload across a contiguous chronological sequence of hundreds of thousands of routine background packets.

The data modality fed into these state-space architectures heavily dictates and artificially restricts their sequence length requirements. The literature relies on three main abstraction techniques:
      1. Raw Bytes to Images: Models like NetMamba transform the raw five-tuple payload and headers into fixed-dimensional (  ) grayscale images to perform masked modeling.23 This visual abstraction completely destroys the chronological, step-by-step micro-temporality of a continuous packet stream.
      2. Header-Only Strides: MeeDet ingests strictly header-only data (Ethernet, IP, TCP/UDP headers), partitioning the sequence into strides and projecting them into a latent space.1 While this preserves some temporal structure, it is confined entirely to the boundary of a single flow, discarding all cross-flow context.
      3. Graph and Multi-Modal Flow: Norns and related frameworks represent traffic using summarized NetFlow/IPFIX features augmented with discrete, localized packet bytes.7 This approach relies on spatial or statistical aggregation rather than raw, unbroken sequential modeling.
No paper currently feeds an unadulterated, chronologically strict stream of 100,000+ raw PCAP packets (or even chronological header sequences mapping multiple distinct flows) directly into a Mamba sequence dimension to empirically test cross-flow, long-term memory retention.

	Strategic White Space: Validating the "Low-and-Slow Stress Test"
The preceding analysis highlights a glaring, structural omission in contemporary cybersecurity Artificial Intelligence research. The academic and operational communities have universally recognized that Advanced Persistent Threats operate over extended timelines, deliberately exploiting the mathematical "forgetting curve" of traditional sliding-window heuristic engines, LSTMs, and Deep Learning NIDS.1 Furthermore, researchers have explicitly adopted S4, Mamba, RWKV, and RetNet specifically because their algorithmic complexity solves the long-sequence scaling and OOM problem inherent to Transformers.8 Yet, despite acknowledging both the threat model and the architectural solution, researchers continue to benchmark these next-generation state space models using legacy methodologies—truncating traffic into 5-packet bursts 27, summarizing data into aggregated NetFlow graphs 7, or converting payloads into static images.23
This profound methodological inertia leaves the definitive, empirical test of Selective State Space Models entirely untouched. The proposed "Low-and-Slow Stress Test" experiment—inserting a reconnaissance ping at step    and the corresponding C&C payload or exfiltration event at step    within a massive, continuous stream of background PCAP data—represents an unattempted, pristine benchmark in the academic literature.

This exhaustive review confirms that Selective State Space Models, specifically Mamba, have rapidly permeated the Network Intrusion Detection System landscape. Their deployment has successfully demonstrated superior inference efficiency, lower memory footprints, and high accuracy on standard benchmarks when compared to quadratic Transformer architectures and legacy Recurrent Neural Networks.
However, a rigorous gap analysis reveals a profound dissonance between Mamba's theoretical capacity and its empirical application. Current methodologies invariably truncate sequences, summarize flows, or isolate analyses to highly localized, short-context windows, thereby artificially blinding these models to the exact multi-stage, temporally protracted behaviors that define an Advanced Persistent Threat. Consequently, the proposed "Low-and-Slow Stress Test" is not merely novel; it is an urgently required corrective to the current state of cybersecurity benchmarking. Executing this test will decisively bridge the gap between abstract algorithmic theory and the operational reality of hunting dormant threats in enterprise environments, definitively answering whether the era of infinite-context network anomaly detection has finally arrived.
